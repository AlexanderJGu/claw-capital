# AI Bull vs Bear Synthesis — The Intelligence Premium Unwind

## Date: 2026-02-23

## The Debate
- **Bear Case:** Citrini + Alap Shah, "2028 Global Intelligence Crisis" (Feb 22, 2026)
- **Bull Case:** Anonymous rebuttal, "The Bull Case for AI: Why the Doom Loop Won't Sustain"

## Where They Agree (High Conviction Trades)
- SaaS/PE-backed software is dead
- Staffing/intermediation companies get destroyed
- Compute/energy/physical infra wins
- Wealth concentration accelerates dramatically
- The transition will be painful in 2026-2028
- Physical-world labor demand increases (Moravec's Paradox buffer)
- Policy response will eventually come (implies money printing → hard assets)

## Where They Disagree
- Does financial contagion become systemic? Bear: yes (mortgage crisis). Bull: no (bounded, targetable — 5-10M workers, specific metros)
- S&P drawdown: Bear says -38%. Bull implies -15-20%
- Timeline to recovery: Bear doesn't model it. Bull says 3-5 years
- Cost deflation speed: Bull says fast enough to cushion. Bear says too slow for housing

## Key Bull Arguments
1. "Triage era ends" — at 90% cheaper cognitive labor, companies build what they never could justify. 30M small businesses priced out of professional services become buyers
2. "Displacement IS deflation" — same force destroying jobs simultaneously collapses cost of living. Legal services don't get cheaper 5 years after layoffs, they get cheaper immediately
3. Moravec's Paradox as economic buffer — AI automates thinking but can't build. Physical world absorbs displaced workers. Electricians, plumbers, construction, data center buildout
4. Oligopoly vs monopoly at model layer determines outcome. AWS/Azure/GCP precedent: oligopoly pushes surplus to consumers. If model layer becomes monopoly, bear case wins
5. "Hedge your own obsolescence" — white-collar workers uniquely can buy equity in the companies replacing them (unlike Rust Belt factory workers)
6. Sovereign wealth fund as policy solution — Norwegian model applied to AI infrastructure

## Key Bull Weaknesses
1. Housing doesn't deflate (35-40% of budget, physical scarcity floor)
2. Bull's own math: non-housing costs drop 25-30%, but with 40% income drop, still a 20-25% real gap
3. "Policy bridge is non-optional" — concedes bear case wins if policy fails
4. Competitive pass-through assumes competitive markets; healthcare/education are oligopolistic
5. SMB demand expansion is slower than enterprise displacement
6. 5-10M workers permanently fall through — the bull case admits this

## Key Bear Arguments (from Citrini piece)
1. Ghost GDP — output in national accounts that never circulates through real economy
2. Negative feedback loop with no natural brake: AI improves → layoffs → less spending → more AI investment → AI improves
3. PE-backed software defaults → private credit → insurance (Apollo/Athene) → "permanent capital" myth
4. Prime mortgage stress — 780 FICO borrowers structurally impaired, not from fraud but from permanent income displacement
5. Tax receipts collapse (government revenue is a tax on human time) while spending needs rise
6. Policy paralysis from partisan gridlock

## Key Bear Weaknesses
1. Timeline too compressed (2 years to 10.2% unemployment is aggressive)
2. Ignores supply-side deflation arriving simultaneously with displacement
3. Doesn't model new demand creation (expanding pie)
4. Underweights Moravec's Paradox as labor absorption buffer
5. Doesn't model recovery scenario
