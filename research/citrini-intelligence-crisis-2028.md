## 2. The Kill Chain

### Stage 1: Agentic Coding â†’ SaaS Collapse
AI coding agents reach the point where they can build, maintain, and iterate on enterprise software autonomously. Why pay $150/seat/month for ServiceNow when an agent builds a custom solution for $0.03? SaaS companies built on **"friction with a friendly face"** â€” making necessary-but-unpleasant workflows tolerable â€” lose their reason to exist. The UI moat dissolves because agents don't need UIs.

### Stage 2: Intermediation Destruction
Every layer of the economy that exists to **coordinate, translate, route, or manage** human activity becomes redundant. This isn't just software â€” it's consulting firms, staffing agencies, marketing platforms, payment processors, gig economy coordinators. The entire service economy is built on intermediation.

### Stage 3: White-Collar Layoffs
Unlike blue-collar automation (which is slow, physical, regulated), white-collar displacement happens at software deployment speed. Millions of knowledge workers lose jobs not because they're bad at their jobs, but because their jobs were **fundamentally about intermediation** that AI handles natively.

### Stage 4: Ghost GDP
National accounts show rising productivity and output. But the output is generated by AI systems that don't earn wages, don't pay rent, don't buy SaaS subscriptions, don't eat at restaurants. GDP grows while the **circulatory system of the economy** (wages â†’ spending â†’ revenue â†’ wages) breaks down. Traditional economic indicators give false comfort.

### Stage 5: PE Software Defaults
Private equity loaded up on software companies at peak multiples with massive leverage, on the thesis that recurring SaaS revenue is "permanent capital." Zendesk ($10.2B H&F/Permira LBO) is the poster child. When enterprise customers cancel subscriptions, the "permanent" revenue evaporates while the debt remains. The **permanent capital myth** shatters.

### Stage 6: Private Credit / Insurance Daisy Chain
PE firms funded software LBOs through private credit vehicles. Those same PE firms (Apollo/Athene, KKR/Global Atlantic, Brookfield/American Equity) also acquired insurance companies to get access to "permanent" float for investing. When PE portfolio companies default, losses flow through to insurance balance sheets. Policyholders â€” retirees with annuities â€” are suddenly exposed to software LBO credit risk they never signed up for.

### Stage 7: Prime Mortgage Stress
White-collar layoffs hit the prime mortgage borrower â€” the $200K+/year knowledge worker in Palo Alto, Austin, or New York. These are the borrowers with 780 FICO scores and $1.2M mortgages. When they lose jobs and can't find equivalent work (because the jobs don't exist anymore), prime mortgage delinquencies spike for the first time since 2008.

### Stage 8: Fiscal Crisis
Government revenues collapse (fewer employed taxpayers, lower corporate profits in service sectors). Spending pressures explode (unemployment insurance, retraining programs, potential UBI). The fiscal math breaks, especially at the state/local level where income and sales tax bases erode simultaneously.

## 3. Key Concepts

### Intelligence Premium Unwind
Every company, role, and asset that commands a premium because it involves "intelligence work" (analysis, coordination, judgment, communication) sees that premium evaporate as AI commoditizes intelligence itself. The $300K consultant, the $150/seat SaaS tool, the $60/sqft Class A office â€” all priced on an intelligence premium that AI destroys.

### Ghost GDP
Output in national accounts that never circulates through the real economy. AI produces more with fewer humans, but the "more" doesn't translate into wages, consumer spending, or tax revenue in the traditional transmission mechanism. Macro indicators diverge from lived economic reality.

### Habitual Intermediation
Layers of economic activity that exist because humans historically needed coordination, translation, and routing. Much of the service economy is habitual intermediation â€” it persists because "that's how it's always been done," not because it adds irreplaceable value. AI exposes the distinction between value creation and value intermediation.

### Friction-with-a-Friendly-Face
The business model of most B2B SaaS: take an inherently frictional process (expense reporting, IT ticketing, CRM data entry) and make it slightly less painful with good UI/UX. The moat is that switching costs exceed the annoyance of the friction. AI eliminates the friction entirely, making the "friendly face" irrelevant.

### Agent-on-Agent Violence
When AI agents on both sides of a transaction negotiate directly, they strip out every intermediary layer simultaneously. An AI procurement agent talking to an AI sales agent doesn't need Salesforce, doesn't need a human SDR, doesn't need ZoomInfo for lead data, doesn't need DocuSign for contracts. The entire B2B software stack collapses.

### Permanent Capital Myth
PE firms marketed insurance float and locked-up fund structures as "permanent capital" â€” money that never leaves, providing stability for long-duration investments. The Citrini piece shows how this permanence is illusory: when underlying investments (software LBOs) blow up, the "permanent" capital takes losses that flow through to policyholders and LPs, triggering redemptions and regulatory intervention.

## 6. Oracle's Assessment

**Directionally right.** The kill chain logic is sound â€” each step follows plausibly from the prior one. The key insight that "AI working = bad for economy" is genuinely underappreciated by the market, which assumes AI = productivity = growth = bullish.

**Timeline too compressed.** The piece models this happening in ~2 years (mid-2026 to mid-2028). More likely this plays out over 4-6 years. Enterprise software contracts have 3-5 year terms. White-collar displacement will face political/regulatory friction. PE structures have legal protections that slow contagion. The kill chain is real but each link takes longer than modeled.

**Best Citrini piece to date.** The synthesis of SaaS disruption â†’ labor displacement â†’ financial contagion â†’ fiscal crisis is original and rigorous. Most AI analysis stops at "some jobs will be lost." This traces the full second and third-order effects through the financial system.

**Key uncertainty:** The piece assumes AI agents reach full production capability in 2026. If this is delayed to 2028-2029, the entire timeline shifts right proportionally. The kill chain logic holds regardless of when it starts.
